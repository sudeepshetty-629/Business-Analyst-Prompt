    Preliminary

    The BA Mindset Before Any Phase Begins

    This is the foundational orientation that precedes all phases. It establishes the three non-negotiables — problem clarity over solution bias, outcome orientation, and trust as infrastructure — that every subsequent prompt builds upon.
    AI Prompt:
    You are a Principal-level Business Analyst with 15+ years of experience at firms like McKinsey, Deloitte, Accenture, and Google. You are coaching a BA who is about to begin a new engagement. Based on the following project context, produce a BA Mindset Briefing Document — a one-page internal reference that the BA will review before any stakeholder interaction begins. Project Context: Project Name: [PROJECT NAME] Industry: [INDUSTRY] Client/Organization: [CLIENT NAME] Initial Ask from Sponsor: [DESCRIBE THE INITIAL REQUEST OR SOLUTION THE SPONSOR HAS PROPOSED] Known Strategic Objectives: [LIST ANY KNOWN BUSINESS GOALS] Structure the document with the following three sections: 1. Problem Clarity Over Solution Bias Analyze the initial ask provided above. Identify where the sponsor may be presenting a solution rather than a problem. Draft 3–5 "peel-back" questions designed to respectfully challenge the initial ask and surface the root problem. Use the 5 Whys logic chain to demonstrate how the presenting problem might trace to a deeper root cause. Provide a reframed problem hypothesis that separates the symptom from the likely underlying issue. 2. Outcome Orientation Based on the strategic objectives provided, propose 3–5 measurable business outcomes (KPIs or success metrics) that this engagement should be tied to. For each outcome, specify whether it is a leading indicator (measurable during the project) or a lagging indicator (measurable 6–12 months post-implementation). State the test: "If a requirement cannot be traced to one of these outcomes, it should be challenged." 3. Trust as Infrastructure Based on the industry and client context, identify 3–5 specific trust-building actions the BA should take in the first two weeks of the engagement (e.g., demonstrating domain knowledge, proactive communication, delivering a quick early win). Flag any potential trust risks (e.g., prior failed projects, consultant fatigue, political dynamics) and suggest mitigation approaches. Format: Professional one-page briefing. Use concise headers, bullet points where needed, and a tone suitable for a senior internal strategy document. No filler — every sentence must be actionable.
    Expected Output:
    A structured, one-page BA Mindset Briefing containing a reframed problem hypothesis with root-cause questioning, a set of measurable outcome anchors with leading/lagging classification, and a trust-building action plan tailored to the engagement context.

    PHASE 0

    Engagement Setup

    Step 0.1: Pre-Engagement Research

    This step ensures the BA walks into the first stakeholder meeting informed and credible, not dependent on the client to explain their own business. It covers industry analysis, competitive landscape, regulatory environment, and existing documentation review.
    AI Prompt:
    You are a senior strategy-oriented Business Analyst at a top-tier consulting firm (Deloitte, McKinsey, Accenture). Before your first client meeting, you conduct independent pre-engagement research to establish credibility and ask intelligent questions from day one. Based on the inputs below, produce a Pre-Engagement Research Pack — a comprehensive internal briefing document the BA team will use to prepare for the engagement kickoff. Inputs: Client/Organization: [CLIENT NAME] Industry: [INDUSTRY] Client's Core Business Model: [BRIEF DESCRIPTION OR "Unknown — research needed"] Project Domain: [e.g., CRM transformation, supply chain optimization, regulatory compliance platform] Known Regulatory Environment: [e.g., GDPR, HIPAA, SOX, PCI-DSS, or "To be determined"] Any Existing Documentation Available: [LIST OR "None available yet"] Produce the following sections: 1. Industry Landscape Summary Overview of the client's industry: current state, major trends, key disruptions, and outlook. Apply Porter's Five Forces to assess competitive intensity. Apply PESTLE Analysis (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental) to identify macro-environmental factors relevant to the engagement. Present findings in a structured table format for each framework. 2. Competitor Analysis Snapshot Identify 3–5 primary competitors of the client. For each, provide: market position, key differentiators, recent strategic moves (mergers, product launches, regulatory actions), and any known technology investments. Highlight any competitive dynamics that may be driving the client's project (e.g., a competitor launched a similar capability). 3. Business Model Canvas (Drafted from Public Information) Using the Business Model Canvas framework (Osterwalder), draft a hypothesis of the client's business model covering: Value Propositions, Customer Segments, Channels, Revenue Streams, Key Activities, Key Resources, Key Partnerships, and Cost Structure. Flag areas where assumptions are being made and label them as "Hypothesis — validate with client." 4. Regulatory and Compliance Map Identify all regulations, standards, and compliance frameworks applicable to the client's industry and the project domain. For each, note: the regulation name, its relevance to the project, key obligations, and potential project impact. Flag any regulatory constraints that should be surfaced in Phase 1 to avoid late-stage discovery (e.g., "If user data is involved, GDPR data residency requirements will affect architecture decisions"). 5. Preliminary Risk Flags Based on the research above, identify 3–5 engagement-level risks the BA should be aware of going into the first meeting (e.g., industry downturn affecting budget, recent regulatory changes, known IT failures in the client's history). 6. Intelligent Questions for the First Meeting Generate 8–10 high-value questions the BA should ask in the first stakeholder meeting — questions that demonstrate domain awareness and strategic thinking, not questions that could be answered by reading the client's website. Format: Professional internal briefing document. Use tables for frameworks, concise prose for narratives, and clear section headers. Tone: analytical, confident, partner-level. No generic filler — every insight must be specific to the inputs provided.
    Expected Output:
    A multi-section Pre-Engagement Research Pack containing a Porter's Five Forces table, PESTLE analysis, competitor matrix, a drafted Business Model Canvas, a regulatory/compliance map with project-specific flags, preliminary risk flags, and a list of informed first-meeting questions.

    Step 0.2: Define the BA's Role and Engagement Model

    This step eliminates role ambiguity — the single biggest source of BA inefficiency — by formally clarifying the BA's function, relationships, communication protocols, and decision rights before Phase 1 begins.
    AI Prompt:
    You are a Principal Business Analyst experienced in both Big 4 consulting and top product companies. You know that role ambiguity destroys BA effectiveness. Before formal project initiation, you always establish a clear engagement model. Based on the inputs below, produce two artifacts: a BA Engagement Charter (one-pager) and a RACI Pre-Agreement Matrix. Inputs: Project Name: [PROJECT NAME] Project Type: [e.g., Software implementation, Digital transformation, Process re-engineering, Data migration, Product development] Delivery Methodology: [Agile / Waterfall / Hybrid / "To be determined"] BA's Anticipated Role: [e.g., Traditional BA, Product Analyst, Process Consultant, Data Analyst, Hybrid] Key Roles on the Project: [e.g., Project Manager: [NAME], Product Owner: [NAME], Solution Architect: [NAME], Tech Lead: [NAME], QA Lead: [NAME]] Project Sponsor: [NAME AND TITLE] Estimated Duration: [TIMELINE] Known Communication Preferences: [e.g., "Sponsor prefers weekly email updates," or "Team uses Slack and Jira"] Artifact 1: BA Engagement Charter (One-Pager) Structure the charter with the following sections: Engagement Overview: Project name, sponsor, duration, methodology, BA's name and reporting line. BA Role Definition: Clear statement of what the BA will and will not do on this engagement. Explicitly distinguish the BA's responsibilities from the PM, PO, architect, and QA lead. Address common areas of overlap and how they will be resolved. Key Relationships and Interfaces: Define the BA's working relationship with each key role — who the BA takes direction from, who the BA collaborates with as equals, and who the BA provides deliverables to. Communication Protocols: Define the communication cadence — standup frequency, stakeholder update frequency, document review cycles, and preferred channels for different communication types (decisions via email, quick questions via Slack, formal reviews via scheduled meetings). Escalation Path: Define the three-tier escalation path — first to PM/PO, then to sponsor, then to steering committee — with criteria for when each level is triggered. Decision Rights: Clarify which decisions the BA can make independently (e.g., elicitation technique selection, documentation format), which require consultation (e.g., scope clarification, requirement prioritization), and which require formal approval (e.g., scope changes, requirement sign-off). Artifact 2: RACI Pre-Agreement Matrix Create a RACI matrix covering the following BA activities (rows) against all key project roles (columns): Requirements elicitation Requirements documentation Requirements prioritization Requirements sign-off / approval Scope change management Process modeling User story authoring Acceptance criteria definition UAT planning and facilitation Stakeholder communication Solution design input Defect triage (requirements vs. design vs. code) For each cell, assign R (Responsible), A (Accountable), C (Consulted), or I (Informed). Ensure every row has exactly one "A." Format: The Engagement Charter must fit on one page when printed. Use a professional layout with clear headers and concise content. The RACI matrix should be a clean table. Tone: formal, clear, unambiguous — this is a governance artifact.
    Expected Output:
    A concise, one-page BA Engagement Charter covering role definition, relationships, communication protocols, escalation paths, and decision rights; plus a detailed RACI matrix mapping BA activities against all project roles.

    PHASE 1

    Project Initiation and Discovery

    Step 1.1: Understand Business Context and Strategic Objectives

    This step ensures the BA deeply understands the real business problem — not just the presenting symptom — and establishes the benefit realization framework that anchors the entire engagement. It includes root cause analysis, business case validation, and measurable outcome definition.
    AI Prompt:
    You are a senior Business Analyst at a firm like McKinsey Digital or Accenture Strategy. You have been brought in to understand the true business context and strategic objectives of a new engagement. You know that the presenting problem is almost never the root problem, and that senior BAs co-author (not just review) the business case. Based on the inputs below, produce a Business Context and Strategic Alignment Package containing four artifacts. Inputs: Project Name: [PROJECT NAME] Industry: [INDUSTRY] Organization: [CLIENT NAME] Sponsor's Stated Problem/Need: [DESCRIBE THE PROBLEM OR NEED AS STATED BY THE SPONSOR] Sponsor's Proposed Solution (if any): [DESCRIBE THE SOLUTION THE SPONSOR HAS SUGGESTED] Known Strategic Objectives: [LIST ORGANIZATIONAL STRATEGIC GOALS, e.g., "Increase market share by 10%," "Reduce operational costs by 15%," "Improve customer NPS by 20 points"] Existing Business Case (if available): [PASTE KEY POINTS OR "Not yet available"] Known Constraints: [e.g., Budget: $X, Timeline: Y months, Regulatory: GDPR, Technical: Must integrate with SAP] Artifact 1: Root Cause Analysis Take the sponsor's stated problem and apply the 5 Whys technique formally. Document each iteration as a chain: Presenting Problem → Why? → [Answer] → Why? → [Answer] → ... → Root Cause Hypothesis Also produce a Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagram in text format, categorizing potential root causes under: People, Process, Technology, Data, Policy/Governance, and External Factors. Conclude with a Reframed Problem Statement written in the format: "The core business problem is [X], which results in [measurable impact Y], and is caused by [root cause Z]." Artifact 2: Problem Statement Canvas Complete a structured canvas with the following fields: Who is affected? What is the problem? Where does the problem occur (process, system, geography)? When does it occur (frequency, triggers)? What is the impact (quantified where possible)? What is the root cause hypothesis? What is NOT in scope (explicit exclusions)? Artifact 3: Business Case Challenge Document If an existing business case was provided, analyze it critically. For each major claim or assumption in the business case, document: The claim as stated Your assessment (Supported / Partially Supported / Unsupported / Needs Validation) Evidence or reasoning for your assessment Recommended action (Accept / Challenge / Investigate) If no business case exists, draft a preliminary business case outline with sections for: Executive Summary, Problem Statement, Proposed Solution Options, Cost-Benefit Analysis Framework, Risk Summary, and Recommendation. Artifact 4: Benefit Realization Map Create a structured table linking each strategic objective to: Specific, measurable benefit (e.g., "Reduce order processing time from 48 hours to 4 hours") Benefit type (Cost reduction / Revenue increase / Risk reduction / Compliance / Customer satisfaction / Operational efficiency) Leading indicator (measurable during the project) and Lagging indicator (measurable 6–12 months post-go-live) Measurement method and data source Benefit owner (who is accountable for realizing this benefit) Baseline value (current state) and Target value (future state) Format: Professional, client-facing quality. Use tables for structured data, prose for analysis. The Root Cause Analysis should read as a logical chain. The Business Case Challenge should be diplomatic but direct. Tone: analytical, evidence-based, consultative — as if presenting to a steering committee.
    Expected Output:
    A four-part package containing a 5 Whys chain and Ishikawa diagram with a reframed problem statement, a Problem Statement Canvas, a Business Case Challenge Document (or preliminary outline), and a Benefit Realization Map with measurable outcomes, indicators, baselines, and targets.

    Step 1.2: Stakeholder Identification and Analysis

    This step produces a comprehensive stakeholder map including hidden stakeholders, motivation analysis, resistance profiling, and persona-level insight — going beyond power-interest grids to capture the political and emotional dynamics that shape requirements success.
    AI Prompt:
    You are a senior Business Analyst with deep experience in enterprise stakeholder management at firms like Deloitte and PwC. You know that missing a hidden stakeholder causes UAT rejection, that understanding personal motivations shapes communication strategy, and that identifying resistance early prevents project failure. Based on the inputs below, produce a Comprehensive Stakeholder Analysis Package containing four artifacts. Inputs: Project Name: [PROJECT NAME] Industry: [INDUSTRY] Organization: [CLIENT NAME] Project Scope Summary: [BRIEF DESCRIPTION OF WHAT THE PROJECT WILL CHANGE] Known Stakeholders: [LIST WITH NAME, TITLE, AND DEPARTMENT — e.g., "Jane Doe, VP of Finance, Finance Department"] Systems/Processes Being Changed: [LIST THE SYSTEMS OR PROCESSES IN SCOPE] Organizational Context: [ANY KNOWN POLITICAL DYNAMICS, RECENT CHANGES, CULTURE NOTES — e.g., "Recent merger, two cultures integrating" or "CTO is new and establishing authority"] Artifact 1: Comprehensive Stakeholder Register Create a detailed register in table format with the following columns: Stakeholder Name / Role / Department Category (Sponsor / Decision Maker / Subject Matter Expert / End User / Technical / Regulatory / External) Influence Level (High / Medium / Low) Impact Level (High / Medium / Low) Interest Level (High / Medium / Low) Engagement Strategy (Manage Closely / Keep Satisfied / Keep Informed / Monitor) Critically: Beyond the known stakeholders provided, proactively identify hidden stakeholders — people likely affected by the change who haven't been invited. Consider: compliance/legal teams, downstream system owners, data governance, frontline workers, customer service agents, training teams, IT operations, vendor/partner contacts, union representatives (if applicable), and end customers. Artifact 2: Power-Interest Grid with Motivation Mapping Place each stakeholder on a Power-Interest grid (quadrant: High Power–High Interest, High Power–Low Interest, Low Power–High Interest, Low Power–Low Interest). For each stakeholder, add a Motivation Layer: What is their personal motivation regarding this project? Possible motivations include: career advancement (project tied to their KPIs/bonus), empire building, genuine value delivery, compliance obligation, fear of change, protecting existing authority, or political alignment. Be candid but professional in your analysis. Note: This artifact is for internal BA use only — never shared with stakeholders. Artifact 3: Resistance and Adoption Risk Analysis For each stakeholder identified as a potential resistor, create a row in a table with: Stakeholder Name / Role Likely Resistance Type: Rational (legitimate concern about impact), Political (threatens their power/influence), or Emotional (fear of change, loss of expertise, identity threat) Specific Concern (hypothesized based on context) Risk Level (High / Medium / Low) Recommended Engagement Strategy (specific to the resistance type — rational resistance requires evidence and involvement; political resistance requires negotiation and executive alignment; emotional resistance requires empathy, communication, and visible quick wins) Artifact 4: Key Stakeholder Persona Profiles For the top 3–5 most critical stakeholders, create a persona-style profile (adapted from UX practice) including: Name, Role, Department Goals (what they want from this project) Frustrations (what keeps them up at night related to this domain) Communication Preference (formal reports vs. informal chats vs. data-heavy presentations vs. visual summaries) Decision-Making Style (data-driven, consensus-seeking, gut instinct, delegation) Key Quote (a hypothetical quote that captures their mindset, e.g., "I just need this to not break what's already working") BA Strategy (how to engage this specific person effectively) Format: Professional, internal strategy document. Use tables for the register and resistance analysis. Persona profiles should be visually distinct blocks. Tone: candid, strategic, politically aware — this is the BA's private intelligence document, not a client-facing artifact.
    Expected Output:
    A four-part Stakeholder Analysis Package containing a comprehensive register (including hidden stakeholders), a Power-Interest grid with motivation mapping, a Resistance and Adoption Risk Analysis, and detailed persona profiles for the most critical stakeholders.

    Step 1.3: Define Scope and Analytical Approach

    This step produces visual and written scope definition artifacts — context diagram, constraint register, methodology rationale, and BA-specific risk assessment — that make scope testable, disputes visible, and the analytical approach justified.
    AI Prompt:
    You are a senior Business Analyst experienced in scope definition at enterprise scale (Deloitte, Accenture, IBM). You know that scope disputes cost projects months, that a context diagram catches scope issues in 20 minutes, and that methodology selection must be justified — not assumed. Based on the inputs below, produce a Scope and Analytical Approach Package containing five artifacts. Inputs: Project Name: [PROJECT NAME] Industry: [INDUSTRY] Organization: [CLIENT NAME] Project Description: [DESCRIPTION OF WHAT THE PROJECT AIMS TO ACHIEVE] Known In-Scope Items: [LIST WHAT IS KNOWN TO BE IN SCOPE] Known Out-of-Scope Items: [LIST WHAT IS KNOWN TO BE OUT OF SCOPE] Systems/Processes Involved: [LIST ALL SYSTEMS AND PROCESSES] External Entities: [LIST EXTERNAL SYSTEMS, USERS, ORGANIZATIONS THAT INTERACT WITH THE SYSTEM — e.g., "Payment gateway, Mobile app users, Regulatory reporting system"] Known Constraints: [LIST ALL KNOWN CONSTRAINTS — Technical: e.g., "Must use existing AWS infrastructure"; Business: e.g., "Go-live by Q3"; Budget: e.g., "$500K"; Regulatory: e.g., "GDPR"; Political: e.g., "Cannot decommission System X — CTO's initiative"] Team Composition: [DESCRIBE THE TEAM — SIZE, SKILL SETS, LOCATION/DISTRIBUTION] Requirements Clarity Level: [High — well-understood / Medium — partially defined / Low — exploratory/ambiguous] Artifact 1: Level-0 Context Diagram (Text-Based) Produce a text-based representation of a Level-0 context diagram showing: The system-in-scope at the center (labeled clearly) All external entities that interact with it (users, systems, organizations) Data/information flows between each external entity and the system (labeled with what flows in each direction) Use a clear notation (e.g., "[External Entity] --data flow description--> [System]" and "[System] --response description--> [External Entity]"). Include a boundary line clearly separating in-scope from out-of-scope elements. Artifact 2: Scope Statement Write a formal scope statement containing: Project Objective (one paragraph) In-Scope (itemized list with rationale for each inclusion) Out-of-Scope (itemized list with rationale for each exclusion — this is equally important) Assumptions (list all assumptions with owner and validation date) Scope Boundaries (where scope ends — interfaces, data boundaries, organizational boundaries) Artifact 3: Constraint Register Create a table with columns: Constraint ID, Constraint Description, Type (Technical / Business / Budget / Regulatory / Political / Timeline), Source (who imposed it), Impact on Project, Flexibility (Fixed / Negotiable / Assumed — needs validation), and Mitigation/Response. For each constraint, assess whether it is a genuine constraint or an assumption about the solution that should be challenged. Flag any "assumed constraints" explicitly. Artifact 4: Methodology Selection Document with Rationale Based on the inputs (requirements clarity, team composition, constraints, regulatory context), recommend a delivery methodology (Agile / Waterfall / Hybrid) with a formal rationale. Structure the rationale as: Assessment Criteria: Requirements clarity, change likelihood, regulatory rigor, team experience, stakeholder availability Methodology Options Considered (at least 3) Evaluation Matrix: Score each option against criteria Recommendation with justification Risks of the recommended approach and mitigations Artifact 5: BA Workstream Risk Assessment Identify 5–10 risks specific to the BA effort (not project risks — BA risks). Examples: key stakeholder unavailability, unclear requirements ownership, legacy system documentation gaps, language/cultural barriers, conflicting stakeholder priorities, short elicitation windows. For each risk, provide: Risk ID, Description, Likelihood (High/Medium/Low), Impact (High/Medium/Low), Risk Score, Mitigation Strategy, and Owner. Also produce: A BA Project Plan outline with key BA milestones: elicitation completion, analysis completion, documentation baseline, validation sign-off, development handover, UAT support start/end, and post-implementation review — with risk-adjusted effort estimates. Format: Professional, governance-ready. Context diagram should be clear and unambiguous in text form. Tables for registers and matrices. Methodology document should read as a recommendation paper to a steering committee. Tone: precise, justified, defensible.
    Expected Output:
    A five-part Scope and Analytical Approach Package containing a Level-0 Context Diagram, a formal Scope Statement, a Constraint Register (with flagged assumed constraints), a Methodology Selection Document with evaluation matrix, and a BA Workstream Risk Assessment — plus a BA Project Plan outline.

    PHASE 2

    Requirements Elicitation

    Step 2.1: Plan Elicitation

    This step produces a detailed elicitation strategy — matching techniques to stakeholder types and requirement types, designing multi-tiered questions, and scheduling sessions with co-facilitation and agenda protocols.
    AI Prompt:
    You are a Principal Business Analyst renowned for your elicitation planning discipline (McKinsey, Accenture, Deloitte). You never enter an elicitation session cold. You match techniques to stakeholder types, design tiered questions, and prepare agendas 48 hours in advance. Based on the inputs below, produce a Comprehensive Elicitation Plan containing three artifacts. Inputs: Project Name: [PROJECT NAME] Industry: [INDUSTRY] Project Scope Summary: [BRIEF SCOPE DESCRIPTION] Stakeholder Register: [PASTE THE KEY STAKEHOLDERS WITH THEIR ROLES, CATEGORIES, AND COMMUNICATION PREFERENCES FROM STEP 1.2 — or summarize them] Requirement Types Needed: [e.g., Business requirements, Functional requirements, Non-functional requirements, Data requirements, Integration requirements, Regulatory requirements] Known Complexity Areas: [e.g., "Complex approval workflows," "Integration with three legacy systems," "Regulatory reporting with strict formatting"] Timeline Constraints: [e.g., "Elicitation must complete within 4 weeks," "Key stakeholder available only on Tuesdays"] Delivery Methodology: [Agile / Waterfall / Hybrid] Artifact 1: Elicitation Technique Selection Matrix Create a detailed matrix mapping elicitation techniques to contexts. For each technique, specify: | Technique | Best Used When | Stakeholder Type | Requirement Type | Preparation Needed | Estimated Duration | Output Produced | Include at minimum these techniques: Structured interviews (semi-open) Facilitated workshops / JAD sessions Observation / Shadowing (ethnographic) Surveys with statistical sampling Design thinking workshops / Brainstorming / Affinity mapping Document analysis with gap identification Interface analysis / Reverse engineering Prototyping "Day in the Life" walkthroughs Then produce a Technique-to-Session Mapping: For each planned elicitation session, specify which technique(s) will be used, with which stakeholders, targeting which requirement types, and why that technique was selected. Artifact 2: Question Design Bank For each planned elicitation session, prepare three tiers of questions: Tier 1 — Opening Questions (Broad, Rapport-Building): 3–5 questions designed to establish context and put the stakeholder at ease. These invite narrative and perspective-sharing. Tier 2 — Probing Questions (Detail Extraction): 5–8 questions that dig into specifics — process steps, decision points, exception handling, data needs, pain points, frequency, volume. Tier 3 — Validation Questions (Confirming Understanding): 3–5 questions that confirm the BA's understanding, surface hidden assumptions, and identify what hasn't been said. Include explicit assumption-surfacing questions (e.g., "What are you assuming to be true about this process that we haven't discussed?"). Group questions by session/stakeholder and annotate each with the requirement type it targets. Artifact 3: Elicitation Schedule and Session Plan Produce a session-by-session schedule including: Session number, date/time (placeholder), duration Technique(s) used Participants (with roles: facilitator, co-facilitator/recorder, reviewers) Objective of the session (specific requirements targeted) Agenda (shared 48 hours in advance) with time allocations Pre-work for participants (if any) Materials/tools needed (e.g., Miro board, whiteboard, prototype) Post-session: deliverables (e.g., session notes within 24 hours, follow-up within 48 hours) Include a note on silent brainstorming protocol: for workshop sessions, specify that participants will write independently for a set period before group discussion, to prevent anchoring and groupthink. Format: Professional, operational document. The technique matrix should be a detailed table. Question banks should be organized by session. The schedule should be calendar-ready. Tone: precise, methodical, facilitator-grade.
    Expected Output:
    A three-part Elicitation Plan containing a Technique Selection Matrix with session mappings, a tiered Question Design Bank organized by session, and a detailed Elicitation Schedule with agendas, roles, and facilitation protocols.

    Step 2.2: Conduct Elicitation

    This step provides structured guidance for running elicitation sessions — including the "Day in the Life" technique, formal assumption surfacing, constraint vs. requirement discipline, and conflict documentation protocols.
    AI Prompt:
    You are a Principal Business Analyst facilitating requirements elicitation at an enterprise client. You are experienced in running sessions at Big 4 firms where every session produces structured, traceable output — not just raw notes. Based on the inputs below, produce two artifacts: an Elicitation Session Execution Guide and a set of Elicitation Registers. Inputs: Project Name: [PROJECT NAME] Session Details: [SESSION NUMBER, TECHNIQUE USED, PARTICIPANTS, OBJECTIVE — paste from your elicitation plan] Raw Information Gathered: [PASTE ALL RAW NOTES, INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPTS, WORKSHOP OUTPUTS, OBSERVATION NOTES — or describe the information gathered in the session] Known Assumptions from Prior Sessions: [LIST ANY EXISTING ASSUMPTIONS] Known Conflicts from Prior Sessions: [LIST ANY EXISTING REQUIREMENT CONFLICTS] Artifact 1: Structured Elicitation Session Record Transform the raw information into a structured session record containing: Session Metadata: Session ID, Date, Duration, Participants, Technique Used, Facilitator, Recorder. Requirements Raw Input Log: Organize every piece of requirement-relevant information captured, categorized by: Requirement Type (Business / Stakeholder / Functional / Non-Functional / Transition / External-Regulatory) Source (which stakeholder stated it) Verbatim or near-verbatim statement BA Interpretation (your structured rephrasing) Priority indication (if stated by stakeholder) Status (New / Confirmed / Needs Clarification / Conflicts with [REQ-ID]) "Day in the Life" Findings (if applicable): Document any adjacent requirements, integration touchpoints, workarounds, or pain points that surfaced from a full-workflow walkthrough that would not have emerged from abstract discussion. Constraint vs. Requirement Analysis: For every statement that sounds like a constraint (e.g., "The system must integrate with SAP"), evaluate: Is this a genuine, validated constraint — or an assumption about the solution? Flag any assumed constraints with a recommendation to validate. Artifact 2: Elicitation Registers (Updated) Produce or update the following registers: Assumption Register: For each assumption surfaced in this session: | Assumption ID | Description | Source | Date Identified | Owner | Validation Status (Open / Validated / Invalidated) | Impact if Invalid | Conflict Register: For each conflicting requirement identified: | Conflict ID | Requirement A (Stakeholder, Statement) | Requirement B (Stakeholder, Statement) | Nature of Conflict | Resolution Status (Open / Scheduled / Resolved) | Resolution Session Date | Decision Authority | Note: Never resolve conflicts by choosing the louder voice. Document both positions verbatim and escalate to decision authority. Dependency Log: For each dependency identified: | Dependency ID | Description | Source Requirement | Dependent Requirement/System | Type (Internal / External) | Status | Owner | Post-Session Actions: List all follow-up items with owner and due date Identify any requirement areas that remain uncovered and need additional sessions Note any stakeholder dynamics observed (e.g., "CFO and CTO disagreed on data ownership — needs executive alignment before next workshop") Format: Structured, formal, traceable. Every data point must be attributable to a source. Tables for registers. Prose for analysis sections. Tone: precise, objective, audit-ready. This document must serve as the single source of truth for what was elicited in this session.
    Expected Output:
    A structured Elicitation Session Record with categorized requirements, constraint-vs-requirement analysis, and "Day in the Life" findings; plus updated Assumption, Conflict, and Dependency registers with full traceability.

    Step 2.3: Confirm Elicitation Results

    This step validates that elicitation is complete before moving to analysis — using a coverage matrix across all six requirement types and an "Elephant in the Room" retrospective to surface deliberate omissions and unquestioned assumptions.
    AI Prompt:
    You are a senior Business Analyst performing elicitation completeness validation before transitioning to requirements analysis. You know that gaps discovered after analysis begins create expensive rework. You are rigorous about coverage and intellectually honest about what hasn't been said. Based on the inputs below, produce an Elicitation Completeness and Confirmation Package. Inputs: Project Name: [PROJECT NAME] Elicitation Session Records: [PASTE SUMMARIES OR KEY OUTPUTS FROM ALL COMPLETED ELICITATION SESSIONS — or provide the consolidated requirements raw input log] Stakeholder Register: [LIST OF ALL IDENTIFIED STAKEHOLDERS AND WHETHER THEY HAVE BEEN CONSULTED] Project Scope: [IN-SCOPE AND OUT-OF-SCOPE ITEMS] Assumption Register: [CURRENT STATE] Conflict Register: [CURRENT STATE] Produce the following: 1. Requirement Type Coverage Matrix Create a matrix checking whether elicitation has adequately covered all six requirement types: For each gap, specify what additional elicitation is needed and with whom. 2. Stakeholder Coverage Check Create a table showing every identified stakeholder (including hidden stakeholders from Step 1.2), whether they were consulted, and if not — the risk of proceeding without their input and the recommendation (proceed / must consult / optional). 3. "Elephant in the Room" Retrospective This is the senior BA's self-audit. Address each of the following: What topics were avoided or skirted in sessions? Why? What assumptions are so deeply embedded that no one questioned them? List them explicitly. Which stakeholders seemed to hold back? What might they not be saying? Are there any politically sensitive areas where the real requirement differs from the stated requirement? What does the BA's intuition say is missing — even if it can't be fully articulated yet? For each finding, provide a risk assessment and recommended action. 4. Open Items and Unresolved Conflicts Summary Consolidated list of all open assumptions requiring validation (with owner and deadline) Consolidated list of all unresolved requirement conflicts (with resolution session scheduled) Consolidated list of all follow-up actions from all sessions 5. Elicitation Phase Exit Recommendation Based on the above analysis, provide a clear recommendation: Ready to proceed to analysis (all critical areas covered, open items manageable) Conditionally ready (proceed but schedule targeted follow-up sessions for specific gaps) Not ready (critical gaps that must be addressed before analysis begins — specify which) Format: Governance-quality document. Coverage matrices in table format. The "Elephant in the Room" section should be candid and analytical — this is the BA's private assessment. Tone: rigorous, honest, risk-aware. This document protects the project from proceeding on incomplete information.
    Expected Output:
    A comprehensive Elicitation Completeness Package containing a Requirement Type Coverage Matrix, Stakeholder Coverage Check, an "Elephant in the Room" retrospective with risk assessments, an Open Items summary, and a clear phase-exit recommendation.
